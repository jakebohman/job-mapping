Methodology

How the map turns raw job postings into a number you can trust: where the data comes from, the corrections that keep it honest, and what it can and can't tell you.

Relative to its size, where is a metro hiring hard right now?

A raw job count just redraws the population map: New York and Los Angeles on top, always, teaching you nothing. So every shaded metro carries one number instead, job postings per 1,000 workers. A reading of 20 means that for every 1,000 people in the local workforce, about 20 distinct openings are currently advertised. Higher points to a tighter, busier hiring market for the metro's size. It is a rate of advertised demand, not a count of jobs and not a measure of employment.

Where the data comes from

Three free, public sources. Nothing is bought, scraped from behind a login, or made up.

A "metro" is a Metropolitan Statistical Area, the government's definition of a city plus its commuting region, built from a set of counties. Counties are the glue: a posting reports its county, an MSA is a list of counties, and BLS reports by MSA, so everything lines up.

How the number is built

For each metro:

  1. Ask Adzuna how many postings are near the metro. The search returns a total count and a sample of 50 actual postings.
  2. Keep only the ones truly inside the metro. Adzuna searches a circle around a city, but a metro isn't a circle, so the circle spills into neighbors. The fraction of the sample reporting an in-metro county is how much of the count we keep.
  3. Remove the reposts. Aggregators carry the same opening many times. Collapsing postings that share a title and employer in the sample gives the fraction that are distinct.
  4. Divide by the workforce, scale to per-1,000.
rate = (raw count × share truly in the metro × share not a repost) ÷ workforce × 1,000

The three honesty corrections

  1. Counts, not a sample. Adzuna's results are ranked by a few big advertisers, so analyzing a sample lets one hospital chain make a whole city look 90% healthcare. A total count can't be skewed that way: it's a census, not a poll. This is the single most important design choice.
  2. Share truly in the metro. Because the search is a circle, a small metro next to a big one can accidentally measure its neighbor. Keeping only the in-metro fraction fixes the geography. Without it, Columbus, Indiana would report Indianapolis's postings.
  3. The repost correction. Roughly half of aggregator postings are duplicates. Applying the distinct fraction keeps the same job posted ten times from inflating the rate. It's a lower bound, since it can merge genuinely separate openings at one employer.
When a metro is left gray. A metro shows no number when it can't be measured honestly: too few postings actually inside it (fewer than ~50 effective), too little of the search sample landing inside it (under 10%, usually a small metro swamped by a large neighbor), or no official workforce figure. Graying these is deliberate; the map declines to show a number it can't stand behind. Puerto Rico is excluded because the data provider returns unreliable locations for it.

What it can't tell you

  • It measures advertised demand, not employment or hires; lots of postings can mean growth or high turnover.
  • Postings over-represent high-churn work (retail, driving, healthcare staffing) that gets re-advertised constantly.
  • It's built on one provider (Adzuna); another would give somewhat different absolute levels. Read the map for relative intensity and rank, not as a precise census.
  • Workforce figures lag by a couple of months, normal for official data.
  • In the sector ranking, healthcare rarely appears even though it's the largest sector in nearly every metro. The ranking surfaces what is distinctive about a metro, not what is merely large.
